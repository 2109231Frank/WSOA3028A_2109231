INFORMATION GEOGRAPHIES

In Graham et al’s “Toward a study of information geographies”, Graham, Sabbata and Zook discuss the inequalities of the generation of information across the globe by linking their arguments to geography. These arguments or points are being substantiated and influenced by political, geographical, social and societal issues. The core question of this piece is where and from whom does the information originate, where does this information go, where and why is it changed or altered and finally what does it all lead to.
Graham explains that there is massively uneven access to certain modes of communication or digital medias, uneven geographies of participation in information generation and huge inequalities in representation in said information. These issues are more often than not amplified as technology advances and new forms of communication arise, since certain geographical hotspots don’t have access to a lot of the “base” forms of communication in the world today. This problem plays back to the question of where the information if coming from seeing that there are geographical spots that contribute little to no information into the world. People, businesses and services that do not contribute to modern information get left behind or become invisible to the rest of the world that do in fact receive and contribute to modern information (for example: businesses in rural areas that do not put themselves on the internet will be “invisible” to those of us that search for businesses on google).
Geographical information distribution has been a mode of power formation and control, explains Graham. This can be explained by maps; when you look at different types of maps over the years, we can see that they are heavily influenced by the politics and the distributed information at the time of generation, some of these maps will have distorted or non-existent views on geographical spots that contribute less to information generation and distribution. The paper later explains how these constraints manifest in the representation of the dominant. In other words, through the generation of information these dominant representations control, in a sense, political, social, and economic power. Graham explains that it is possible in our digital age that older hierarchies of dominant representation could be broken down or democratised seeing that the ease of use of modern forms of media could be a radical change in the “political economy of information”. In other words, through modern forms of media citizens and consumers play a more direct and important roll in the shaping, mediating, and augmenting of the information that is consumed and distributed, this in turn removes some power from the more traditional mediators of information, and thus allows less inequality in the generation, distribution, and representation of/in information. In theory this should open up different geographies of participation and representation that are more equal across the globe than it has ever been.
In this paper the authors look at three main types of geographies, namely: geographies access and enablement, geographies of participation and geographies of representation. Access to the internet and digital technologies lie at the heart of these three geographies. The geographical figures shown in the paper show us that first world countries have high percentages of access to the internet (more citizens) and more third world countries have little access and in some geographical spots almost no access. Through data provided we can clearly see the inequalities across the globe with regards to information access, contribution, and representation. The paper explains that the core observations is that this geographical inequality is being reinforced by new information technologies rather being alleviated by them. In order to change this inequality, we need to, according to Graham, deconstruct, disentangle the mutability and immutability of information, map, and integrate the geographies of information and finally we are going to need to ask critical questions about power. This paper reveals that the geographical information inequalities are not changing any time soon, but the paper does point out the key areas where we need to change our information distribution, creation and representation.
